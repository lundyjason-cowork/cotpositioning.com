Positioning Signals Are Not Trade Recommendations
The signals generated by this dashboard identify statistically extreme divergences in futures positioning between trader categories. They reflect where the smart money is leaning — not where price is going tomorrow. A positioning signal is one input in a multi-factor decision framework. A COT long signal on a commodity that is technically breaking down, in a macro regime that doesn't support it, with deteriorating fundamentals, is not a trade — it's a data point. These signals identify asymmetry in positioning. The trader's job is to determine whether that asymmetry translates into asymmetry in expected returns.

Edge #1
OI-Adjusted Positioning

A 50,000-contract net long when total open interest is 200K means 25% of the market is positioned one way. The same 50,000 when OI is 500K is only 10%. Raw net positioning treats them identically — we normalize first.

Conventional
Z = (Net - Mean) / StdDev
Idx = (Net - Min) / (Max - Min)
➔
This Dashboard
Ratio = Net / OI
Z = (Ratio - Mean) / StdDev
Idx = (Ratio - Min) / (Max - Min)

This adjustment applies to both the Z-score and the 0–100 Index. It eliminates false signals from secular market growth and catches genuinely extreme positioning that raw numbers miss in high-OI environments.

Edge #2
Z-Scores Are Self-Healing

A traditional min-max index is hostage to outliers. One blowout week anchors the min or max and stretches the entire range wide, compressing normal readings into a narrow band. Everything looks moderate even when positioning is historically extreme.

When that outlier eventually rolls out of the lookback window, nothing changes in the market — but the range suddenly contracts around normal values, and the index can jump to an extreme overnight. That's not a signal — it's a formula artifact.

Z-scores are self-healing. As outliers roll through the window, the mean and standard deviation adjust gradually — no cliff-edge recalculation. A reading of −3.5σ is a statistically meaningful statement, not a rank that flips because one data point aged out.

Edge #3
Z-Spread: Cross-Category Signal

We don't score a single category in isolation. The signal fires on the Z-spread — the divergence between managed money and commercials:

Z-Spread = Z(MM Net/OI) − Z(COM Net/OI)
Long signal fires when Z-Spread ≤ −threshold
Short signal fires when Z-Spread ≥ +threshold

When the spread exceeds the threshold, these two groups are more divergent than 99%+ of the lookback history. Commercials hedge physical exposure; managed money follows trend. When they lean in opposite directions at statistical extremes, that's where asymmetry lives.

Edge #4
0-100 Index Confirmation

The Z-score drives the signal. The OI-adjusted 0–100 Index is the independent confirmation layer. They measure different things — the Z-score captures deviation from the mean, the Index captures position within the range. Agreement between the two increases conviction.

An Index signal fires when Managed Money falls below 5 and Commercials rise above 95 (long setup), or the inverse (short setup). When the Z-spread triggers and the Index confirms, you know the reading isn't a statistical artifact — two independent lenses agree that positioning is at an extreme.

When they diverge — Z fires but Index doesn't confirm — it's a flag to look more carefully before acting. The signal is present but lacks the second vote of confidence.

Edge #5

A single lookback window forces a trade-off between capturing secular trends and staying responsive. We run three concurrent windows with calibrated thresholds — shorter windows demand higher thresholds because they're noisier:

Trader Categories Used
Our signals are computed on three CFTC-reported categories: Managed Money (trend-following funds and CTAs), Commercials (producers, merchants, and processors who hedge physical exposure), and Institutional / Asset Managers (pension funds, endowments, and insurance companies). We intentionally exclude Other Reportables (small speculators) from signal generation — this category is a noisy catch-all of smaller traders whose positioning lacks the informational value of the primary three groups and can dilute signal quality.

How We Validated These Signals
Every signal on this dashboard has been backtested across 10 years of CFTC positioning data (2015–2025) covering 37 futures contracts across all sectors. We tested forward returns at 1, 2, 4, 8, and 13 weeks after each signal fired. The process ran in four phases: signal combination optimization, threshold optimization, out-of-sample validation, and crypto-specific calibration. The thresholds shown in the Methodology section above are the direct output of this work.
Phase 1
Signal Architecture Selection

We defined 8 atomic signal components and tested all 252 valid combinations to find the optimal signal architecture:

#ComponentDescription
1–3Z-Spread (2yr, 1yr, 6mo)MM minus COM OI-adjusted z-score exceeds threshold
4–6Index Extreme (2yr, 1yr, 6mo)MM ≤ 5th / COM ≥ 95th percentile (or inverse)
7MM/COM Individual Z ≥ ±2Both sides individually extreme on all 3 timeframes
8INST Non-ConflictInstitutional positioning not contradicting signal direction

Each combination was evaluated independently for buy and sell sides. Signals were deduped (same contract/direction within 4 weeks kept only the first), ensuring each signal represents an independent event.

Phase 2
Combination Optimization Results

The scoring metric was win_rate × avg_return averaged across all forward windows — favoring signal quality (high hit rate with meaningful returns) over signal quantity.

Best buy combo: Z-Spread (all 3 TFs) + Index Extreme (all 3 TFs). The full 6-of-6 architecture captures secular positioning extremes with index confirmation. Adding MM/COM individual z-scores or INST non-conflict as additional filters marginally improved win rates but cut signal count significantly.

Best sell combo: Same 6-of-6 structure, but sell-side returns were consistently weaker than buy-side — motivating the use of stricter sell thresholds (Phase 3).

Crypto contracts were analyzed separately due to their short positioning history and extreme volatility. Their signals showed higher variance but the same directional edge.

Phase 3
Threshold Optimization

With the signal architecture locked, we swept Z-spread thresholds from 1.5σ to 5.0σ (step 0.25) for each timeframe independently, then ran a full grid search across all three timeframes simultaneously.

We enforced a monotonic constraint: 2yr < 1yr < 6mo — shorter lookback windows demand stricter thresholds because the rolling standard deviation is estimated from fewer observations and is noisier.

The sell side requires stricter thresholds on shorter windows because short-side positioning extremes are inherently noisier — managed money crowded-long positions tend to be more persistent and informative than crowded-short positions, which can reflect hedging activity rather than directional conviction.

Phase 4
Crypto-Specific Threshold Optimization

Crypto contracts (BTC, ETH, SOL, XRP) have fundamentally different positioning dynamics than traditional futures. We ran an unconstrained optimization — no monotonic constraint, thresholds swept 1.0σ to 5.0σ (step 0.25) — across single-TF, two-TF, and three-TF combinations independently.

Data constraints: BTC has ~410 weeks of history (all 3 timeframes), ETH ~254 weeks (all 3 TFs), but SOL (~51 weeks) and XRP (~42 weeks) only have 6-month data. Multi-TF analysis is effectively BTC + ETH only.

Buy Side: 2yr Is the Only Timeframe That Matters
The 2-year z-spread is the binding constraint. At 2yr ≥ 4.25σ, only 3 signals fire (BTC Mar 2020, BTC Oct 2024, ETH Oct 2024) — all massive winners averaging +49.4% at 8 weeks. Lowering to 2yr ≥ 2.75σ gives 8 signals with +20.1% at 4 weeks and 88% win rate. The 1yr and 6mo thresholds are non-binding — same signals fire regardless of what you set them to.
Sell Side: Lower Thresholds Work Better (Opposite of Non-Crypto)

Why the inversion? In traditional futures, extreme sell-side positioning (crowded longs) is informative because unwinding is slow. Lower thresholds capture the earlier, more actionable phase of positioning divergence before extremes are reached.

Statistical Context: What Do These Thresholds Mean?

Under a normal distribution, a 3.0σ event occurs 0.13% of the time (once in ~770 observations). A 4.0σ event is 0.003% (once in ~31,500). A 4.5σ event is even rarer — roughly 1-in-300,000.

In practice, CFTC positioning data has fat tails — these extreme z-scores fire more frequently than normal theory predicts. That's precisely why they carry informational value: when positioning reaches these extremes despite the fat-tailed distribution, the divergence between managed money and commercial hedgers is genuinely historic.

The escalating threshold structure (lower thresholds for longer windows, stricter for shorter) compensates for estimation noise: a 26-week rolling standard deviation is computed from half the data points of a 104-week window, making the z-score noisier. Requiring a higher threshold for shorter windows ensures the signal clears the noise floor.

Caveats & Limitations

Survivorship bias: Only contracts currently listed are tested. Delisted contracts (which may have had poor signal characteristics) are excluded.

Regime dependence: The 2015–2025 period includes QE, COVID, and rate-hike cycles. Future regimes may produce different results.

Transaction costs not included: Returns are gross of commissions, slippage, and financing costs. Real-world execution will reduce edge.

Signal frequency: These are rare signals by design (~11 buy signals/year, ~4 sell signals/year). They identify positioning asymmetry, not trade entries — confluence with technicals, macro, and fundamentals is required.
